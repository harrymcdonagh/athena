### Development Process

Pharmaceutical development is time-consuming, expensive, and risky; very few candidates become approved medicines. The process from discovery to regulatory approval exceeds a decade. Candidates can fail at any development stage; even late-stage candidates sometimes fail approval or commercial success. Novel modalities present more challenging development timelines.

**Discovery Phase:** Scientists identify, design, and synthesize promising candidates by analyzing effects on biological targets. Only candidates expected to have desired effects move to animal studies. This phase can take years with extremely low probability of any candidate becoming medicine.

**Early Development Phase:** Initial safety and efficacy testing and manufacturing requirement analysis. Safety testing occurs in laboratory and animal studies; Phase 1 testing in small human participant groups assesses safety and dosing; Phase 2 testing in larger patient populations identifies efficacy signs while assessing safety. Scientists identify safe, effective, economical manufacturing processes. Long-term animal studies continue. Only a fraction of early-stage candidates advance to late development. Duration varies, potentially taking several years.

**Late Development Phase:** Phase 3 candidates have met initial safety requirements and shown efficacy evidence; they have higher success likelihood than earlier candidates. Trials involve large patient populations to demonstrate safety and efficacy, often compared to competitive therapies or placebo. Studies are conducted globally, are costly, and support regulatory filings. Duration varies by disease state, potentially taking years.

**Submission Phase:** After regulatory agency submission, approval timing varies from months to several years depending on disease state, data strength and complexity, unmet need degree, and regulatory agency evaluation timeline (affected by prioritization and other factors). No guarantee of approval or consistent decisions across geographic areas exists.

# Risk Factors Summary — Eli Lilly and Company (10-K, 2025)

## Business and Industry Risks

### R&D Complexity and Product Pipeline Dependency
Pharmaceutical R&D is costly and highly uncertain with a high failure rate. Products may fail at any development stage despite substantial investment. Lilly must continuously replenish its product portfolio to replace revenues lost to patent expiration, generic/biosimilar competition, and pricing controls. The company notes that **Mounjaro and Zepbound accounted for 56% of total revenues in 2025**, with six products (Mounjaro, Zepbound, Verzenio, Trulicity, Taltz, and Jardiance/combinations) collectively representing **82% of total revenues in 2025**. New safety or efficacy data post-approval may result in label changes, product recalls, or withdrawals. Safety issues may spawn costly product liability claims. Concerns about products in the same class or with similar mechanisms of action could adversely impact Lilly's products or candidates.

Lilly depends on intellectual property protection for most revenues, cash flows, and earnings. **Trulicity will lose significant patent and remaining data protections in the next few years.** Generic entry typically results in rapid and severe revenue declines. ### Global IP Landscape Threats

An EU agreement-in-principle announced in December 2025 would revise pharmaceutical legislation to make conditional the length of certain IP incentives, including reductions in maximum data protection periods. U.S. officials have proposed limitations on patent numbers and scope. Competitors may assert infringement claims against Lilly. Weak IP protection in certain jurisdictions creates heightened risks of generic and counterfeit competition at or shortly after launch.

### Cybersecurity and Data Protection
Lilly relies on complex IT systems to store and transmit confidential information, including trade secrets, intellectual property, clinical trial data, and personal information. Cyber-attacks are increasing in frequency, sophistication, and intensity. Pharmaceutical manufacturing is complex and highly regulated. Supply chain difficulties may arise from Lilly's operations or external environmental factors (tariffs, cost inflation, labor market dynamics, natural disasters, pandemics, geopolitical tensions). **The company and the pharmaceutical industry depend on China-based suppliers for portions of the supply chain, including integral chemical synthesis, reagents, starting materials, and ingredients.** Finding alternative suppliers may be infeasible or require significant time and expense due to regulatory approval requirements. Overestimation of demand for expanded manufacturing capacity could render built or in-process capacity unnecessary and result in significant contractual payment obligations.

### Third-Party Outsourcing
Lilly outsources clinical trial management, product testing, manufacturing, and distribution to contract research organizations, contract manufacturers, and logistics providers. Third parties may fail to perform to standards, produce unreliable results, breach confidentiality, experience IT disruptions, or fail to satisfy commitments. Lilly deploys AI in various operations and has entered partnerships on AI-related drug discovery.
